Reasoning vs Size

When to use a reasoning model, when to scale up model size, and how to navigate the crossover points — for intermediate LLM users.

Section 01

The Two Axes

Model selection has two largely independent levers. Understanding what each actually does prevents the mistake of defaulting to "bigger is better" when thinking is needed, or vice versa.

More Parameters (Size)
Compresses more world knowledge and pattern associations into weights. A larger model has seen more, knows more, and generalises better across diverse domains — but still answers in a single forward pass.
Reasoning / Extended Thinking
Adds inference-time compute. The model explicitly works through intermediate steps before answering — spending more tokens to plan, verify, and self-correct. This is orthogonal to raw size: a small model with thinking can beat a larger model without it on structured problem-solving.
Remember
These two dimensions are orthogonal and can be combined. Haiku + extended thinking, Sonnet + adaptive thinking, Opus + adaptive thinking are all distinct operating modes with distinct cost/quality profiles. The question is always which combination makes sense for a specific task.

Section 02

When Reasoning Wins

Reasoning (extended/adaptive thinking) earns its cost premium in specific structural conditions. Below are the conditions with the strongest signal — if your task matches several of these, turn thinking on.

1
Five or more sequential steps Research shows reasoning models outperform non-reasoning equivalents by ~17% when chain-of-thought requires 5+ steps, but only ~3% for under 5 steps. Under 3 steps, reasoning models can underperform due to overthinking.
2
Verifiable correctness matters Math, code logic, formal proofs, algorithm correctness, constraint satisfaction. The model can self-check intermediate steps — which matters when wrong is worse than slow.
3
Novel problem structure Problems the model can't pattern-match from training — unfamiliar combinations, unusual constraints. Reasoning helps the model construct rather than recall an answer.
4
Strategic / agentic planning Multi-step plans with dependencies, decision trees, or backtracking. Reasoning models excel at decomposing goals and managing sequential uncertainty — OpenAI recommend o-series for agent orchestration.
5
Debugging complex systems Tracing failures through layered systems — distributed code, multi-step pipelines, logical contradictions. The explicit chain-of-thought makes errors easier to spot and correct.
6
High-stakes decisions with low error tolerance Research suggests reasoning models outperform on cost-adjusted accuracy as soon as the economic cost of a mistake exceeds ~$0.01. For high-value decisions, the premium pays for itself.
Pro Tip
Use reasoning mode as a planner feeding into a non-reasoning executor. This hybrid pattern — reason once to produce a plan or decision, then execute with a faster model — captures most of the accuracy benefit at a fraction of the full cost.
Section 03

When Bigger Wins

More parameters means more knowledge, finer stylistic control, and better generalisation — without the latency tax of inference-time compute. These are the cases where going up a tier beats turning on thinking.

1
Knowledge-intensive tasks Factual recall, domain expertise, synthesis across sources. Larger models know more — reasoning cannot manufacture knowledge it wasn't trained on. Sonnet over Haiku+thinking for knowledge-heavy tasks.
2
Creative and stylistic work Writing, tone matching, nuanced rewriting, poetry, brand voice. Reasoning can make outputs feel mechanical and over-structured. Larger models have richer stylistic range without the thinking overhead.
3
Complex instruction following Long system prompts, multi-constraint outputs, persona maintenance across a conversation. Larger models track complex rules more reliably without explicit step-by-step reasoning.
4
Long-context comprehension Analysing large codebases, long documents, or extended conversation history. Opus 4.6/4.7 and Sonnet 4.6 have 1M token context vs Haiku 4.5's 200K — and more parameters to make sense of that context.
5
Latency-sensitive applications Real-time chat, voice agents, interactive tools. Reasoning's thinking phase delays time-to-first-token significantly. Sonnet without thinking is faster than Haiku with thinking for interactive use.
6
Structured output reliability JSON generation, function calling, consistent format adherence. Research and practitioner experience suggest non-reasoning models produce more consistent structured outputs — reasoning mode can introduce variability.
When to Use
A useful heuristic: if you could solve the task by being very knowledgeable without particularly deliberating, bigger model wins. If you could solve it by thinking carefully step by step even without deep domain knowledge, reasoning mode wins.

When to Use

Section 05

Reasoning Model Watch Outs

Reasoning mode is not a universal upgrade. Several well-documented failure modes can make it worse than a non-reasoning equivalent — know these before defaulting to "turn thinking on."

Watch Out
Overthinking on easy tasks. Research (THOUGHTTERMINATOR paper, 2025) found reasoning models are poorly calibrated on simple problems — they generate large amounts of redundant tokens that don't improve accuracy. On very easy tasks, reasoning models underperformed GPT-4o in ~24% of cases. Use thinking only when the task genuinely warrants it.
Watch Out
Reasoning hallucinations. Reasoning models can produce "logically coherent but factually incorrect" chains — the confident step-by-step format makes these harder to detect than standard hallucinations. Multiple studies (Tsinghua/NUS 2025, Renmin University 2025) found reasoning models show increased factuality degradation on knowledge benchmarks like SimpleQA compared to their non-reasoning counterparts.
Watch Out
Structured output inconsistency. Reasoning mode can introduce variability in structured outputs (JSON, function calls, schemas). For reliable structured generation, non-reasoning models with explicit format instructions tend to be more consistent. DeepSeek V3.1 explicitly disables reasoning for function calling.
Watch Out
Visual/multimodal degradation. Research (RH-Bench, 2025) found that longer reasoning chains reduce the model's attention to visual inputs, amplifying visual hallucinations in multimodal tasks. Use reasoning mode cautiously when vision is load-bearing.
Watch Out
Don't add CoT prompting to reasoning models. Reasoning models already perform chain-of-thought internally — adding explicit "think step by step" instructions is redundant and can interfere. Keep prompts minimal: state the task, constraints, and desired output format. Limit few-shot examples to one or two.
Watch Out
Context budget. Thinking tokens count against your context window. Haiku 4.5's 200K limit can be consumed quickly by large thinking budgets on long-context tasks. Sonnet 4.6 and Opus 4.6/4.7's 1M window is more permissive, but interleaved thinking across many tool calls can still accumulate unexpectedly.
Section 06

Prompting Adjustments by Mode

The same prompt that works well for a non-reasoning model often needs adjustment for a reasoning model, and vice versa. These are the key differences.

Prompting Reasoning Models

  • Be minimal. State the task, constraints, and output format. Don't pad with context that isn't structurally necessary — the model will reason through it itself.
  • No explicit CoT instructions. Don't say "think step by step" or "reason carefully" — these are redundant and can inflate token usage without benefit.
  • Few-shot sparingly. One or two examples maximum. More examples don't add value and may constrain the model's reasoning path.
  • Define "done." For agentic tasks, specify what a correct answer looks like and how the model should verify its work — this guides the reasoning toward productive self-checking.
  • Treat reasoning_effort / thinking budget as a tuning knob. Start lower and increase if quality is insufficient — don't default to maximum.
  • Specify constraints explicitly. Reasoning models are good at satisfying stated constraints — the more precisely you specify them, the better the reasoning will honour them.
Don't (reasoning mode)
You are an expert software engineer. Think carefully and step by step about this problem. First, consider all the possible approaches. Then, reason through each one thoroughly before picking the best solution. Here are three examples of how I'd like you to approach this… [3 long examples follow]
Do (reasoning mode)
Refactor this Python function to handle the edge case where the input list is empty. Output: a single corrected function with a docstring. Constraints: must not change the function signature.

Prompting Non-Reasoning Models

  • Explicit CoT is valuable. Add "think step by step" or structure your prompt to elicit intermediate reasoning — this compensates for the lack of built-in thinking.
  • Few-shot examples work well. Non-reasoning models respond strongly to pattern demonstration — use 2–5 examples for format-sensitive tasks.
  • Role / persona framing helps. "You are a senior data analyst" sets a consistent frame that shapes output style and vocabulary across a response.
  • Be explicit about output format. XML tags, JSON schemas, numbered lists — non-reasoning models benefit from precise format specification and will adhere reliably.
  • Use scratchpad patterns. Ask for reasoning in a tagged block before the final answer: <thinking>...</thinking> followed by <answer>...</answer>.
  • Chain prompts for complex tasks. Split multi-step work into sequential prompts rather than asking for everything at once — non-reasoning models are more reliable when scope is bounded.

Hybrid Patterns

Combining reasoning and non-reasoning models in a pipeline often beats using either alone. These patterns are used in production by OpenAI, Anthropic, and their customers.

A
Plan → Execute Use Sonnet+thinking (or Opus) to produce a structured plan or decision. Feed the plan into Haiku (no thinking) for fast, cheap execution of each step. Best for agentic workflows with many routine sub-tasks.
B
Triage → Escalate Route simple queries through Haiku (no thinking). A classifier detects queries needing deeper reasoning and escalates to Sonnet+thinking. Reduces cost while maintaining quality on hard cases.
C
Draft → Refine Use Sonnet (no thinking) for a fast first draft. Use Sonnet+thinking or Opus to critique and refine. Cheaper than starting with the high-end model, better than staying with the cheap one.
D
Verify → Confirm Non-reasoning model produces an answer. Reasoning model checks it for logical consistency or constraint satisfaction. Useful when output must be auditable but generation should be fast.

Pro Tip
When you're genuinely unsure whether thinking helps, test empirically: run a sample of your actual tasks through Sonnet with and without thinking. Measure quality difference, cost difference, and latency difference. For many real-world tasks, the improvement is smaller than expected — and sometimes absent entirely.
Caveat
This landscape changes rapidly. Anthropic releases new models and updates capabilities frequently — for example, Haiku 4.5 only gained extended thinking in October 2025. Always verify current model specs and pricing before building around a specific combination.
